ABSTRACT
Climate change has intensified the frequency and severity of droughts, increasingly threatening environmental 
sustainability by reducing agricultural productivity and placing additional stress on water resources. Although 
accurate drought forecasting is vital for resource management and disaster mitigation, it remains a formidable 
challenge because of the intricate and non-stationary characteristics of climatic and ecological processes. This 
study proposes an innovative hybrid modeling framework that integrates wavelet transform preprocessing with 
ensemble-based machine learning (ML) models, XGBoost, AdaBoost, and Random Forest, to enhance agricultural 
drought prediction. The Sindh Province of Pakistan was selected as the case study area. CHIRPS precipitation 
data and MODIS-derived environmental indicators, including Land Surface Temperature (LST), Normalized 
Difference Vegetation Index (NDVI), Modified Normalized Difference Water Index (MNDWI), Vegetation Con -
dition Index (VCI), and Vegetation Health Index (VHI), were introduced into models. Wavelet-based decompo -
sition was first applied to the time-series data to capture multi-scale patterns and reduce noise. The resulting 
signals were then used to train the ML models to forecast drought, as represented by the VHI. Results show that 
wavelet preprocessing significantly improves prediction accuracy. Among the tested models, XGBoost achieved 
the highest performance (R² = 0.964), followed by AdaBoost (R² = 0.946) and Random Forest (R² = 0.9263). 
These findings highlight the effectiveness of combining wavelet analysis with ML techniques for drought 
assessment. The proposed framework offers a robust decision-support tool for identifying drought-prone areas, 
enhancing agricultural resilience, and informing policy responses to climate-related risks.

in agricultural yields ( Jiang et al., 2025; Wu, 2022 ; X. Zhang et al., 
2024 ). Severe drought conditions significantly reduce chlorophyll levels 
and water accessibility in plants, interfering with photosynthesis 
mechanisms ( Jiang et al., 2023; Li et al., 2025a ; Yue Zhao et al., 2024 ). 
This disruption accelerates the beginning of agricultural drought and 
intensifies declines in agricultural productivity ( Cui et al., 2025a; 
Wright and Kitajima, 2022 ). In the next 30 years, the global population 
is predicted to surge by over 70 % ( Sadiq et al., 2023a ; B. Wang et al., 
2024 ; Xi et al., 2024 ), presenting significant challenges in addressing 
drought severity ( Kang et al., 2024; Smith and Boers, 2023 ). Such in -
creases in drought severity could intensify food insecurity as countries 
struggle to meet the demands of their rapidly growing populations 
( Dunn, 2020; Fang et al., 2024 ; Q. Zhao et al., 2024 ). Droughts can 
trigger conflicts, forced migration, wildfires, food shortages, and

Droughts are influenced by several factors, including variations in 
precipitation, evapotranspiration rates, decreased groundwater flow, 
and imbalances between water requirements and supply ( Du et al., 
2024 ; Li, 2022 ; Qingping Liu et al., 2024 ). Meteorological droughts arise 
from insufficient precipitation, such as inadequate rainfall to sustain soil 
moisture or excessive rainfall that exceeds the soil’s retention capacity 
( Luan et al., 2024a; Sadiq et al., 2023b; Xue et al., 2024; Yu et al., 2022 ). 
Vegetation droughts, on the other hand, occur due to low soil moisture 
during critical growing seasons ( Haq, 2024 ), driven primarily by 
elevated temperatures and excessive evapotranspiration ( Guo et al., 
2023; Haq and Khan, 2022 ; Yue Zhao et al., 2024 ). Vegetation drought is 
among the most prevalent natural disasters, significantly affecting crop 
yields and livelihoods ( Jiang, 2020 ; P. Zhang et al., 2024 ). An insuffi -
cient water supply undermines plant health and productivity, which is 
influenced by factors such as insufficient precipitation, high tempera -
tures, and excessive evapotranspiration ( Du et al., 2024 ; Thomas et al., 
2021 ; Wei et al., 2024 ). However, agricultural drought is also driven by 
complex internal and external automated mechanisms, including cli -
matic variations and human intervention ( Bauters, 2022; Eini et al., 
2024; Manu, 2022; Smith et al., 2023; Vallicrosa, 2023 ).
Droughts occur frequently in Pakistan, at least three times every ten 
years ( Javed et al., 2024 ). The country has faced numerous intense 
drought incidents, mainly affecting the Sindh and Balochistan regions 
( Ansari et al., 2024; Muzammil et al., 2023; Oh et al., 2023 ). Prolonged 
droughts from 1999 to 2002 and 2014–2018 reduced crop yields by 
60–80 %, causing substantial economic losses at both provincial and 
national levels ( Novoa et al., 2023; Rahman et al., 2024 ). Recent years 
have seen growing concern regarding the escalating severity and fre -
quency of droughts in Pakistan, as noted in various other contexts in 
regions of Asia as well ( Ali, 2018; Ali et al., 2023; Yu et al., 2023 ). The 
observed increase in heat waves and extreme weather events in Sindh 
indicates a possible rise in the frequency of droughts ( Alizadeh et al., 
2024; Wu, 2021 ). Hence, it is crucial to employ effective vegetation 
drought monitoring techniques, which are vital for assessing drought 
impacts, developing early warning systems, conducting risk assess -
ments, and mitigating the adverse effects of drought.
Satellite remote sensing has grown in importance for evaluating 
drought because it can detect changes in vegetation health on various 
scales ( Chanuwan Wijesinghe et al., 2024; Moreno-de-las-Heras et al., 
2023 ). This helps decision-makers pinpoint drought-prone areas and 
allocate resources effectively to minimize their effects without relying 
on ground-based observations. Satellite imagery offers comprehensive 
insights, enabling the observation of drought conditions with a high 
spatial accuracy ( Li et al., 2025b; Sadeghi-Jahani et al., 2024; Xi and 
Yuan, 2023 ). Indices derived from remote sensing, such as the Land 
Surface Temperature (LST), Vegetation Condition Index (VCI), Tem -
perature Condition Index (TCI), and Vegetation Health Index (VHI), are 
primarily utilized to assess the effects of water and thermal stress on 
plant growth ( de Oliveira et al., 2022 ; Kafy et al., 2023a ; Putzenlechner 
et al., 2023 ; Y. Wang et al., 2024b ). The VHI relates TCI and VCI to assess 
the overall condition and production of vegetation ( Cui et al., 2025b; 
Ding et al., 2024; Hammad and Falchetta, 2022 ). Different research 
projects evaluated agricultural droughts using Normalized Difference 
Vegetation Index (NDVI), Modified Normalized Difference Water Index 
(MNDWI), the sources of surface water classified as Permanent and 
Temporary Surface Waterbodies (PTSW), and Soil Moisture Content 
(SMC) ( Adeyeri et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2023; Yang et al., 2024 ; D. 
Zhang et al., 2024 ; Yuxin Zhao et al., 2024 ; Zhu et al., 2024 ). The VCI, 
TCI, and VHI are found to be significantly more effective in detecting 
drought than the NDVI, and can minimize the influence of geographic 
factors such as weather, soil, vegetation, and topography ( Luan et al., 
2024b; Weng et al., 2023; Zafar et al., 2025 ).
Understanding ecosystems and climate dynamics necessitates the 
utilization of data-driven approaches to generate precise forecasts for 
the upcoming decades (S. Ali et al., 2022 ; Li et al., 2024 ; Pande et al., 
2024 ; Zafar et al., 2024b ). This includes the use of cutting-edge tech -
niques, including deep learning (DL) ( Foroumandi et al., 2023 ) and 
machine learning (ML) models ( Nourani et al., 2025 ), to predict climate 
variables and the availability of natural resources, such as vegetation 
conditions, under different climate change scenarios ( Huang et al., 
2025 ). A variety of potential models have been developed, including 
bagging, extreme gradient boosting (XGBoost), random forest (RF), 
adaptive boosting (AdaBoost), autoregressive integrated moving 
average (ARIMA) models, artificial neural networks (ANNs), multiple 
linear regression, K-N neighbors, regression trees, and additional 
advanced models. These models have demonstrated outstanding effi -
cacy in predicting climatic variables and various datasets ( Cheng et al., 
2023, 2025; Karbasi et al., 2023; Xiao et al., 2024 ; L. Xu et al., 2024 ). 
Yet, selecting the optimal model frequently relies on identifying distinct 
characteristics and the array of ecological variables being assessed ( He 
et al., 2023 ). Fortunately, remote sensing datasets provide important 
time series data for analysis ( Lomeo et al., 2024; Yu et al., 2023; Zhou 
et al., 2024 ). Although not extensively utilized in machine learning 
models to date, predicting drought vulnerability through VHI via sat -
ellite data holds considerable potential for improving our understanding 
of the effects of climate change and extreme weather events ( Han et al., 
2024; Zhang et al., 2023 ). Over the past decade, wavelet transformation 
has gained widespread recognition as a powerful tool for analyzing time 
series trends, particularly for identifying variations and periodicities in 
data ( Tao et al., 2024; Zheng et al., 2023 ). This approach offers a 
comprehensive representation of signals in both time and frequency 
domains, providing significant insights into the fundamental processes 
and their underlying structural patterns ( Liu et al., 2025; Wu et al., 
2024 ). Wavelet methods have been increasingly adopted by researchers 
for preprocessing time series data inputs, aiming to enhance model 
performance ( Aon et al., 2024 ). This integration addresses a funda -
mental limitation of machine learning models in handling 
non-stationary data effectively ( Guo et al., 2022 ). The initial step in 
creating wavelet-enhanced machine learning models entails preparing 
the original dataset through wavelet transformation ( Khan et al., 2020 ). 
Hybrid models, which combine linear stochastic methods with nonlinear 
machine learning models, can more accurately predict droughts 
( Chidepudi et al., 2023 ; X. Liu et al., 2024 ; F. Wang et al., 2024 ).
The novelty of this study lies in its integrated hybrid framework, 
which uniquely combines discrete wavelet transformation for multi- 
resolution analysis of non-stationary remote sensing data with a 
comparative ensemble of advanced machine learning models, including 
XGBoost, AdaBoost, and RF. This method systematically decomposes 
input signals to enhance feature extraction, utilizing wavelet coherence 
to identify time-frequency relationships among drought indicators, 
thereby improving predictive accuracy for complex dynamics of vege -
tation health. This approach differs from methods that rely on single 
models or raw data. A thorough comparison of various boosted and tree- 
based models for VHI prediction establishes a strong benchmark for 
selecting the most effective algorithm for drought forecasting. The study 
aims to enhance the accuracy of machine learning frameworks for 
drought assessments and forecasting by evaluating the performance of 
hybrid models. Furthermore, this research lays the groundwork for 
identifying key indicators of drought severity and their impact on 
agricultural productivity, ultimately facilitating the improvement of 
early warning and drought monitoring systems to enhance the effec -
tiveness of climate adaptation and mitigation strategies.

The procedure for XGBoost modelling involves several phases. The 
dataset underwent initial preprocessing, which involved data cleaning, 
tackling missing values, and removing outliers. As a result, relevant el -
ements were identified, chosen, and developed to improve the model ’ s 
performance. We also used data transformation methods like standard -
ization and normalization. The pre-processed dataset was used to train 
the XGBoost model, a collection of decision tree models that were 
underperforming. The model ’ s performance was assessed using relevant 
statistical metrics on an independent testing dataset post-training ( Haq,

important features were determined by analyzing feature importance.

model complexity and introducing regularization through a Bayesian 
search over 100 iterations with 5-fold cross-validation. We limited the

configuration (max_depth = 4, learning_rate = 0.05, subsample = 0.8, 
colsample_bytree = 0.8, reg_lambda = 1.5) successfully generalized to 
unseen data.
2.5. Random Forest
Random Forest is a sophisticated ensemble-supervised machine 
learning algorithm that constructs models and produces predictions by 
adapting the Random Forest algorithm ( Eini et al., 2024; Han et al., 
2024 ). The process involves constructing numerous decision trees 
through the bagging technique, utilizing bootstrapped samples. This 
involves generating random samples from the input data and training 
decision trees using these samples. The method forecasts by averaging 
the outputs of separate trees, thereby minimizing overfitting via ma -
jority voting (Quanshan Liu et al., 2024 ). The algorithm guarantees that 
decision trees remain uncorrelated by choosing random subsets of fea -
tures for training. This approach minimizes model variance and im -
proves prediction accuracy, establishing it as a strong option for 
regression and prediction tasks ( Faheem et al., 2024 ).
Bootstrap sampling, also known as bagging, is used when training a 
model. To train separate trees, this means randomly sampling several 
subsets of the data with replacements. At each split, every tree selects a 
random feature subset, generating variation and reducing correlation 
among the trees. The model runs predictions by combining results using 
a majority vote for classification tasks and an average for regression 
tasks once training is finished. Feature importance analysis identifies the 
most critical predictors using Mean Decrease in Impurity (MDI) among 
other measures.
The assessment of model performance includes the use of Mean 
Squared Error (MSE) and R² Score for regression tasks, along with 
metrics like accuracy, precision, recall, and F1-score for classification 
tasks. For the RF model, we directly managed overfitting by tuning 
parameters that promote "weak" and diverse learners through a ran -
domized grid search combined with 5-fold cross-validation. We tested 
various values, including max_depth [10, 15, 20, None] to control tree 
growth; min_samples_split [2, 5, 10] and min_samples_leaf [1, 2, 4] to 
prevent nodes from learning from overly small samples; max_features 
[ ’ sqrt ’ , 0.7, 0.8] to decorrelate the trees; and n_estimators [100, 200, 
300] to ensure enough learners. The best model, with parameters 
max_depth = 15, min_samples_split = 5, max_features = 0.7, and 
n_estimators = 200, showed strong generalization. The ultimate trained 
model is subsequently employed to predict drought, utilizing environ -
mental indicators, including VCI, TCI, LST, NDVI, VHI, MNDWI, and 
precipitation data.
2.6. Adaptive Boosting
Adaptive Boosting (AdaBoost) is a robust ensemble learning algo -
rithm that improves the performance of weak classifiers by sequentially 
modifying weights based on misclassified samples ( Du et al., 2024 ; 
Fankhauser et al., 2022 ; L. Xu et al., 2024 ). AdaBoost enhances models ’ 
precision by integrating several Decision Trees (stumps). At the outset, 
the model distributes uniform weights across all training samples and 
trains the initial weak classifier. Following each iteration, misclassified 
samples are assigned increased weights, enhancing their impact in the 
subsequent training phase ( Zhang et al., 2023 ). The ensemble under -
went iterative training, where each weak learner focused on previously 
misclassified samples and modified the sample weights appropriately. 
Selecting an appropriate weak learner as the base model is essential for 
the effectiveness of the ensemble. An assessment was conducted on 
multiple weak learners to determine their strengths, enabling the se -
lection of the most suitable algorithm based on the results. Methods such 
as oversampling, undersampling, or adjusting class weights were uti -
lized to enhance the model ’ s efficacy in imbalanced data. The outcomes 
of weak learners were amalgamated by weighted selection ( Pande et al., 
2024 ). ensemble to prevent overfitting. We carefully tuned the number 
and contribution of its sequential learners through a comprehensive grid 
search, thereby mitigating the tendency to overemphasize complex, 
hard-to-classify samples. This involved optimizing n_estimators [50, 
100, 200] to determine the optimal number of boosting rounds, lear -
ning_rate [0.1, 0.5, 1.0] to control the contribution of each learner, and 
crucially, the max_depth of the base estimator [1, 2, 3] to ensure the use 
of truly "weak" learners. The final model configuration 
(n_estimators = 200, learning_rate = 0.1, base_estimator__max_depth = 2) 
successfully countered the tendency towards overfitting.

3. Results
3.1. Discrete wavelet transform
This study employed the DWT to conduct a multiresolution analysis 
of critical drought-related variables, including the TCI, VCI, VHI, LST, 
NDVI, MNDWI, and precipitation data. The main aim of utilizing DWT 
was to break down these intricate time series into their fundamental 
components, facilitating the separation of long-term trends from short- 
term variations. A three-level decomposition was conducted utilizing 
the ‘Haar ’ wavelet, which was recognized for its simplicity and effec -
tiveness in interpreting environmental data characterized by sudden 
changes. The decomposition yielded an approximation component that 
signifies the overall trend (low-frequency behavior) and several detail 
components that encapsulate high-frequency fluctuations and anoma -
lies. Fig. 3 depicts the DWT decomposition outcomes for all variables 
examined in the study. Each row in the picture represents an input 
variable, while the columns illustrate the original time series, the 
extracted trend (approximation), and the detail coefficients at decom -
position levels 1, 2, and 3, respectively. The approximation components 
describe the fundamental trends in temperature, vegetation health, and 
water indices over the study period, whereas the detail coefficients 
underscore short-term fluctuations that may relate to seasonal cycles or 
abnormal occurrences such as droughts or excessive precipitation.

3.2. Correlation analysis
The correlation study among the principal drought-related variables 
uncovers numerous significant correlations that interpret the 
interactions between vegetation health, climate parameters, and water 
availability. A robust negative connection was identified between LST 
and TCI (r = -0.96), signifying that elevated surface temperatures 
correlate with heightened temperature stress on vegetation, resulting in 
diminished TCI values LST and the VHI show a strong negative 
connection (r = -0.90), which emphasizes the harmful effect of rising 
temperatures on plant health. In contrast, TCI had a robust positive 
connection with VHI (r = 0.92), indicating that temperature conditions 
significantly affect overall vegetation vitality. As anticipated, the VCI 
and the NDVI demonstrated a robust positive association (r = 0.93), 
given that both indices evaluate vegetation greenness and vigor. NDVI is 
a fundamental component in VCI calculation. Moreover, VCI and VHI 
exhibited a robust correlation (r = 0.75), underscoring the intimate 
connection between vegetation conditions and comprehensive evalua -
tions of vegetation health. Precipitation exhibited a strong positive 
connection with the MNDWI (r = 0.74), signifying that heightened 
rainfall substantially increases surface water availability, as indicated by 
MNDWI values. The correlation results suggest that temperature-related 
variables, especially LST and TCI, significantly affect vegetation health 
in the studied area. Water availability, as evidenced by precipitation and 
MNDWI, influences vegetation indices. The correlation between pre -
cipitation and vegetation indices, specifically NDVI (r = 0.28) and VCI

due to the semi-arid conditions of Sindh, where vegetation growth is 
influenced by irrigation, soil moisture storage, and temperature-driven

conditions using multi-source satellite-derived variables.
4. Discussion
4.1. Overview of key findings and interpretation
This study thoroughly evaluated the effectiveness of three ensemble 
machine learning models, XGBoost, AdaBoost and Random Forest (RF), 
within the framework of satellite-based drought mapping using a variety 
of remote sensing variables. The amalgamation of vegetative (VCI, 
NDVI, VHI), meteorological (LST, precipitation), and hydrological 
(MNDWI) indicators established a thorough modeling framework to 
elucidate drought dynamics across time. The performance measures, 
comprising RMSE, MAE, and R² ( Table 2 ), clearly demonstrated the 
XGBoost model ’ s better efficacy. The model achieves minimal error 
rates and optimal correlation during training and testing. The RF and 
AdaBoost models exhibited commendable performance; however, their 
accuracy slightly declined during the testing phase, indicating possible 
overfitting tendencies when compared to XGBoost. The research utilized 
advanced ensemble models (XGBoost, RF, AdaBoost) in conjunction 
with wavelet-based signal decomposition to enhance the accuracy of 
drought mapping, which contrasts with traditional methods. The 
amalgamation of multi-source satellite indicators facilitated enhanced 
comprehension of droughts ’ intricate and nonlinear dynamics. The 
comparative analysis using Taylor Diagrams ( Fig. 11 ) provided an 
advanced graphical evaluation of model accuracy, variability, and cor -
relation. The training phase diagram ( Fig. 11 -A) revealed that all models 
closely aligned with the observed VHI standard deviation and correla -
tion. However, in the testing phase ( Fig. 11 -B), the XGBoost model 
maintained its proximity to the observed data point, reflecting greater 
robustness and generalization capacity under unseen conditions. In 
contrast, RF and AdaBoost exhibited a wider spread, indicating higher 
errors and variability. The robust performance of XGBoost compared to 
AdaBoost and RF is a result of its unique algorithmic advantages. Unlike 
AdaBoost, which adjusts the weights of misclassified samples, XGBoost

4.2. Computational challenges of integrating wavelet transforms with ML 
in GEE
Implementing the proposed hybrid wavelet – ML framework revealed 
several computational challenges when combining advanced modeling 
techniques with the GEE platform. While GEE offers unparalleled access 
to large-scale satellite archives and efficient preprocessing of remote 
sensing data, its JavaScript-based environment and client-side process -
ing structure impose constraints on performing recursive, in-memory 
calculations required by DWT and ensemble algorithms such as 
XGBoost and AdaBoost. These operations demand iterative computation 
and substantial memory resources, which are not natively optimized 
within GEE.
Consequently, the workflow of this study adopted a two-stage 
configuration. Remote sensing indices (LST, NDVI, VCI, TCI, VHI, and 
MNDWI) together with precipitation data were first extracted and pre -
processed in GEE, after which DWT decomposition, feature engineering, 
model training, hyperparameter tuning, and drought forecasting were 
carried out externally in a Python environment. This hybrid setup

4.3. Practical barriers to real-time implementation
In practice, the transition of this framework from experimental 
modeling to operational real-time drought monitoring remains chal -
lenging. The first barrier lies in data latency: MODIS-derived indicators 
typically have a delay of one to two days, while CHIRPS precipitation 
data may lag by up to three weeks, restricting immediate updates. 
Second, the workflow ’ s reliance on both cloud-based processing and 
local Python environments complicates automation, especially for 
agencies with limited computational infrastructure. Network bandwidth 
and storage constraints can also hinder the frequent reprocessing of 
large spatiotemporal datasets required for real-time systems. Moreover, 
model retraining is necessary to maintain accuracy under changing 
climatic conditions, further increasing computational costs and opera -
tional complexity. Consequently, the framework currently supports 
near-real-time rather than fully real-time drought forecasting.

4.5. Limitations and future work
Although the results are encouraging, this study faces several tech -
nical limitations. MODIS and CHIRPS datasets were selected because 
they provide long-term, consistent, and globally available observations, 
which are essential for drought analysis at regional and temporal scales. 
However, their moderate spatial and temporal resolutions may constrain 
fine-scale assessments and introduce uncertainties in areas lacking dense 
ground validation. Future studies could improve local-level accuracy by 
incorporating higher-resolution data from the Landsat or Sentinel sat -
ellite constellations, thereby enabling more detailed drought charac -
terization and supporting precision agriculture applications.
While ensemble methods inherently mitigate overfitting through 
regularization and model averaging, the high R² values observed in this 
study warrant further verification of model generalization. Future work 
should test the framework on independent datasets and under varying 
climatic conditions to ensure robustness and to assess the transferability 
of model parameters beyond the Sindh region.
This study employed the VHI as the target indicator because of its 
direct relevance to agricultural drought, as it integrates information on 
vegetation vigor and thermal stress. However, relying solely on VHI 
presents certain limitations. The index mainly reflects vegetation re -
sponses to water and heat stress, while other factors such as land man -
agement practices, crop types, and phenological variations can also 
influence vegetation conditions. Furthermore, the study focused pri -
marily on agricultural drought impacts and did not include meteoro -
logical indices such as the Standardized Precipitation Index (SPI) or the 
Standardized Precipitation Evapotranspiration Index (SPEI), nor direct 
measurements of root-zone soil moisture, which is a key determinant of 
plant water availability. This methodological choice was made to 
maintain consistency in using high-frequency, satellite-derived land 
surface data. Future research could address this limitation by integrating 
multiple drought indicators that combine meteorological indices (SPI or 
SPEI), agricultural drought responses (VHI), and soil moisture data to

The models in this study were trained exclusively on data from Sindh

learning framework itself is inherently transferable because it relies on 
remotely sensed indicators that are globally available and comparable

be adapted to other regions with appropriate calibration. Application to

In addition, this study employed ensemble-based machine learning

performance with advanced deep learning architectures, including

5. Conclusion
This study develops a robust framework for drought prediction by 
fusing advanced machine learning with wavelet transform preprocess -
ing, utilizing several critical remote sensing indices, including VHI, 
NDVI, LST, TCI, VCI, MNWDI, and precipitation. The explicit integration 
of the DWT for time-series decomposition proved to be a critical step, 
markedly enhancing the models ’ ability to capture the complex, non- 
linear, and non-stationary patterns characteristic of drought phenom -
ena. Among the evaluated models, XGBoost achieved the highest pre -
dictive accuracy with an R² of 0.964, an RMSE of 0.031, and an MAE of 
0.023, outperforming both Random Forest and AdaBoost. Further 
analysis of variable interactions revealed significant correlations that 
interpret the interplay between climate and vegetation. A notable strong 
negative correlation was identified between LST and the TCI 
(r = – 0.96), indicating that rising surface temperatures intensify ther -
mal stress on vegetation. Similarly, a robust negative correlation be -
tween LST and the VHI (r = -0.90) highlights the negative impact of 
increased temperatures on overall plant health. These findings confirm 
that the models effectively capture critical environmental dynamics. The 
results provide significant new insights for thorough drought moni -
toring, offering practitioners and policymakers essential tools to 
pinpoint vulnerable areas, enhance water management strategies, and 
inform climate change adaptation policies. By enabling more accurate 
forecasting of future drought conditions, this approach helps with pro -
active decision-making to reduce severe social, economic, and envi -
ronmental impacts, thereby supporting a more resilient agricultural 
sector and improving drought preparedness in an evolving world.
